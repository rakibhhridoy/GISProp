Operations & Regulatory

Making It Work in Bangladesh

Operating drones in Bangladesh requires navigating CAAB regulations, security clearances, and operational restrictions. We've built this into our operating model. Let's walk through what's required and how we'll execute.

CAAB Regulatory Requirements

1. Drone Registration

  • Required for drones >248g or range >100m
  • Process: Submit specs, insurance proof, operator credentials to CAAB
  • Timeline: 2-3 weeks
  • Cost: BDT 10,000-20,000 per aircraft

2. Remote Pilot Certification (ROC)

  • Training Partner: Avion Aerospace Academy (CAAB-approved)
  • Requirements: 50+ flight hours, ground school, medical certification (Class 3), written + practical exam
  • Duration: 4-6 weeks per pilot
  • Cost: BDT 8-12 lakhs per pilot (includes all training)

3. Flight Safety Request (FSR)

  • Submit 45 days in advance of every mission
  • Multi-agency clearances: ADOC, DGFI, NSI, Local Police
  • Daylight operations only (30 min before sunrise to 30 min after sunset)
  • Visual Line of Sight (LoS) required—no autonomous flights beyond sight
CAAB regulations are clear but require advance planning. Drone registration is straightforward once pilots are certified. The critical path is pilot certification through Avion Aerospace Academy, which takes 4-6 weeks per pilot and costs BDT 8-12 lakhs. This is why we recruit experienced drone pilots or invest heavily in training our first cohort. The 45-day FSR requirement for each mission means we need to plan campaigns carefully and batch missions to optimize flight scheduling. Daylight-only operations and LoS requirements are standard for this stage of drone regulations globally.

CAAB Operational Categories

Open (Low-Risk)

  • Low altitude
  • Urban areas
  • Less processing
  • Our use: Minimal

Specific (Medium-Risk)

  • Higher altitude
  • Rural areas
  • Risk assessment required
  • Our primary (80% of work)

Certified (High-Risk)

  • Beyond visual range
  • High altitude
  • Complex approvals
  • Our use: Future (Years 2-3)
We'll operate primarily under the "Specific" category, which covers medium-risk operations in rural areas (perfect for agriculture and infrastructure mapping). This requires risk assessment and CAAB approval but is achievable within our timeline. As we mature and invest in beyond-visual-range capabilities, we can move to Certified operations for higher-value missions.

Insurance & Compliance

Insurance Requirements

Third-Party Liability Insurance: BDT 50-100 lakhs minimum

Covers damage to third parties (people, property). Mandatory for all operations. Premium: BDT 3-5 lakhs/year.

Operational Restrictions

  • Altitude: Maximum 120m AGL (above ground level) for Specific operations
  • Distance: Must maintain Visual Line of Sight at all times
  • Speed: Limited to 16 m/s (standard for civil drones)
  • No-Fly Zones: Restricted airspace around airport, government buildings, sensitive areas
  • Weather: No operations in wind >10 m/s or poor visibility

Security Clearances (per FSR)

  • ADOC (Air Defense Operations Center): Airspace clearance
  • DGFI (Directorate General of Forces Intelligence): Security vetting
  • NSI (National Security Intelligence): Sensitivity assessment
  • Local Police: Ground-level coordination
Insurance is non-negotiable and moderately expensive (BDT 3-5 lakhs/year), but it's the cost of operating legally. Security clearances through ADOC, DGFI, and NSI are thorough and take time (45 days is the standard), so we need to plan missions well in advance. These aren't bureaucratic barriers—they're legitimate safety and security measures for a nation managing airspace over 160 million people. We build this into our project planning and cost structures.

Risk 1: Regulatory Changes

Risk:

CAAB tightens regulations, restricts LiDAR operations, or adds new fees

Impact:

Could increase operating costs 20-50%, delay flights, reduce addressable market

Probability:

Medium (30-40%) - CAAB modernizing, but regulations evolving

Mitigation:

  • Engagement: Build relationships with CAAB officials, participate in regulatory discussions
  • Advocacy: Join industry associations, provide feedback on regulations
  • Diversification: Don't rely solely on Bangladesh—prepare contingency for regional expansion (India, Nepal)
  • Flexibility: Build business model that works under Open, Specific, AND Certified categories
Regulatory risk is real but manageable. Bangladesh is actively modernizing drone regulations, not restricting them. Our mitigation is engagement—we build relationships with CAAB officials, provide feedback on regulations, and stay informed. We also build our business model to work under multiple regulatory regimes, not just one.

Risk 2: Competitive Pressure

Risk:

Established competitors (GeoTech, Digital Survey) add Data Science apps to their offerings

Impact:

Reduce our differentiation, increase pricing pressure, slow farmer adoption

Probability:

Medium (40-50%) - Competitors are capable but resource-constrained

Mitigation:

  • Speed: Move fast on farmer apps & NGO partnerships—18-24 month head start
  • Lock-in: Build deep NGO relationships with exclusive deals (difficult to replicate)
  • Data Moat: Accumulate 3+ years of farm data to train better Data Science models
  • Focus: Competitors pursue high-margin government contracts; we focus on farmers (different economics, different channels)
Competitors *will* eventually try to copy our model. But we have a 18-24 month advantage because they're optimized for government contracts (high-value, long sales cycles). Building farmer relationships through NGOs requires a different sales motion, different pricing, different product. By the time they reorganize to compete, we'll have 50,000+ farmers locked in with data network effects. Speed and focus are our defensive moves.

Risk 3: NGO Partnership Failure

Risk:

NGO partners (BRAC, ASA) not interested in app distribution, or distribute poorly

Impact:

Farmer app revenue takes 2+ years longer to scale, business becomes services-only

Probability:

Medium (30%) - NGOs are eager for new revenue, but execution risks exist

Mitigation:

  • Proof of concept: Pilot with small NGO first (5,000 farmers) to prove model works
  • Incentive alignment: Design partnership with NGO commission structure that rewards them for results
  • Backup channels: Develop direct sales team + government school networks as alternative channels
  • Multiple partners: Partner with 3-5 NGOs, not 1-2 (reduce dependency)
NGO partnerships are key to our model but unproven. The best mitigation is a genuine proof of concept—partner with one NGO for a 5,000-farmer pilot, prove the economics work, then expand. We also build backup channels (direct sales, government partnerships) so we're not entirely dependent on NGOs. This is prudent risk management for a business model that requires external partners.

Risk 4: Technical Integration Challenges

Risk:

LiDAR data integration with Data Science models proves harder than expected, models underperform

Impact:

Farmer apps don't deliver promised ROI, adoption stalls, revenue model fails

Probability:

Low-Medium (20%) - Technology is proven, but integration complexity is real

Mitigation:

  • Proof of technology: Validate models on pilot farms in Year 1, before full product launch
  • Expert team: Hire experienced Data Science/GIS engineers from competitive companies or universities
  • Iterative development: Start with simpler models (demand prediction) before complex ones (disease detection)
  • Fallback strategy: Farmer apps can survive on traditional indices (NDVI) + farmer input if LiDAR integration incomplete
Technical risk exists but is manageable. LiDAR + Data Science integration is a known problem (not research-level). The best mitigation is a disciplined engineering process: start simple, validate with pilot farmers, iterate. We don't need perfect models in Year 1—we need "good enough" models that farmers find valuable. Better models come in Year 2-3 as we accumulate more data.

Risk 5: Team & Talent Constraints

Risk:

Difficulty recruiting experienced pilots, Data Science engineers, GIS specialists in Bangladesh

Impact:

Hiring delays slow operations by 3-6 months, require higher salaries, reduce margins

Probability:

High (60%) - Bangladesh tech talent market is competitive

Mitigation:

  • Hire from competitors: Recruit GeoTech, Grihayan pilots/engineers with experience
  • Train internally: Partner with Avion Aerospace for pilot training pipeline
  • Remote hiring: Recruit Data Science engineers from South Asia (India, Pakistan) with competitive salaries
  • Equity incentives: Offer equity to key hires to reduce salary requirements
  • Graduate recruitment: Partner with Bangladesh Agricultural University for early-career talent
Talent risk is high but addressable. Bangladesh has good engineering talent, but it's competitive. Our strategies: hire from competitors (they know our market), train our own pilots through Avion Academy, recruit Data Science engineers from India/South Asia, use equity to attract risk-takers. This is a solvable problem with patience and capital.

Risk 6: Market Adoption by Farmers

Risk:

Farmers reluctant to adopt apps, prefer traditional methods, low willingness-to-pay

Impact:

Farmer app adoption lags, recurring revenue doesn't materialize, business remains services-only

Probability:

Medium (30-40%) - World Bank data shows 127K farmers *already* adopting drones

Mitigation:

  • Proof from World Bank: 127,500+ farmers already using drones proves market wants tech
  • NGO distribution: Through BRAC/ASA, we reach farmers with high trust scores (not cold outreach)
  • Pricing strategy: BDT 5-10K/year is 0.3-1% of farmer income, with 10-30 week payback period
  • Demonstrated results: Show real examples of farmers earning 10-15% more with our apps
  • Freemium model: If needed, offer free version with limited features to drive adoption
This risk is lower than it appears. World Bank data proves farmers *are* adopting agricultural tech and seeing real benefits. We're not trying to convince farmers to adopt technology—they're already doing it. We're offering a better, cheaper, more accessible solution through NGO partners they trust. The risk is execution, not market demand.
